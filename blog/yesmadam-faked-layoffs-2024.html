Case Study: YesMadam Fake Layoffs Controversy (2024)

82%
Backlash Threat Score

In December 2024, home salon service startup YesMadam launched a marketing campaign on LinkedIn that simulated a mass layoff. An email was leaked showing that the startup was terminating employees who reported high stress. The campaign designed to highlight mental health instead backfired, drawing severe condemnation for exploiting employee vulnerability and job loss anxieties.

“Faking employee termination to market mental wellness is a major ethical failure. Shock-based PR that plays on economic and job anxieties destroys corporate credibility.”

1. The Mechanics of Deceptive Shockvertising

The campaign gained traction after an email, allegedly from YesMadam's HR department, circulated online. The text stated that employees indicating "significant stress" in a workplace survey were being let go immediately. This sparked furious debates on professional networks, particularly LinkedIn, where users called out the brand's lack of empathy and basic human rights.

The setup violated **ASCI Chapter I (Honest Representation / Prevention of Deception)**, as it intentionally misled the public to generate shock value. Netizens criticized the startup for mocking actual tech industry layoffs and exploiting their employees' psychological distress to achieve viral reach.

2. Corporate Apology and Re-evaluation

Following intense outrage, YesMadam's CEO, Mayank Arya, issued a public apology. He clarified that no employees were terminated and that the email was part of a planned initiative to introduce their "Happy 2 Heal" campaign and a new policy offering six paid mental health leaves annually.

The primary takeaway from this incident is that brands must respect the boundaries of ethical communication. Staging stressful scenarios or faking corporate misconduct under the guise of raising awareness degrades trust and results in long-term damage to the employer brand.
