Zomato Kachra Campaign Controversy: When Creative Metaphors Cross Caste Sensitivity Lines

How Zomato's recycling campaign using the iconic 'Kachra' character from Lagaan triggered one of the most significant caste sensitivity backlashes in Indian advertising history.

In June 2023, Zomato—India's largest food delivery platform—launched a creative recycling campaign that seemed ingenious on paper but became a textbook example of how creative metaphors can inadvertently reinforce historical oppression.

The Campaign: "Kachra" as Recycling Mascot

Zomato's campaign featured Aditya Lakhia, the actor who played "Kachra" in the iconic 2001 film Lagaan. The creative concept transformed Lakhia into various household items made from recycled materials—a lamp from a bottle, a stool from newspapers—visually demonstrating how "kachra" (garbage) could be repurposed into useful items.

The campaign's tagline played on the dual meaning: "Kachra kachra nahi hota" (Kachra/Garbage is not really garbage).

Creative Intent vs. Cultural Reality

From a creative standpoint, the campaign was clever:

  • Leveraged a beloved Bollywood character for instant recognition
  • Delivered an environmental message about recycling
  • Created visually striking, shareable content
  • Aligned with Zomato's sustainability initiatives

However, the creative team failed to recognize the caste sensitivity implications of using the term "Kachra" in this context.

Why "Kachra" is a Loaded Term in Caste Context

Historical Oppression Through Language

The word "Kachra" (कचरा) in Hindi literally means garbage, trash, or waste. For centuries, this term has been used as a dehumanizing slur against Dalit communities forced into sanitation work due to the caste system.

In many parts of India, Dalit sanitation workers are still referred to as "kachra" or "kachrawala" as a caste-based insult, equating their personhood with garbage.

The Lagaan Connection: Double-Edged Recognition

In the film Lagaan, "Kachra" was a character from the untouchable (Dalit) community who faced severe discrimination but ultimately became a cricket hero. While the film portrayed this positively, the character's name reinforced the very slur used to oppress his community.

Zomato's campaign took this one step further—literally turning a person named 'Garbage' into garbage items. For those familiar with caste-based slurs, the visual of a Dalit-identified character being transformed into household objects made from waste was deeply offensive.

The Backlash: Social Media Eruption

Within hours of the campaign's release, social media platforms—particularly Twitter and Instagram—erupted with criticism:

  • Dalit activists and scholars pointed out the caste insensitivity
  • hashtags like #ZomatoCasteist and #BoycottZomato trended
  • Civil rights organizations issued statements condemning the campaign
  • Academics explained the historical context of "kachra" as a slur
"The campaign inadvertently reinforced centuries of caste-based dehumanization. Turning a person associated with the Dalit community into literal 'kachra' items shows a fundamental lack of cultural awareness."
— Dalit Rights Activist Response

Zomato's Response: Acknowledgment and Apology

Zomato moved quickly to contain the damage:

  • The campaign was pulled within 48 hours
  • CEO Deepinder Goyal issued a public apology
  • The company acknowledged the oversight regarding caste sensitivity
  • Promised more rigorous cultural review processes

Brand Safety Lessons from the Kachra Controversy

1. Language Has Historical Weight

Words that seem neutral or creative to one group may carry traumatic historical weight for others. "Kachra" may seem like just a movie character name to some, but it's a centuries-old slur to others.

2. Metaphors Can Reinforce Oppression

Even well-intentioned metaphors can inadvertently reinforce harmful stereotypes. The visual of transforming a Dalit-associated character into garbage items reinforced the very dehumanization the caste system imposed.

3. Diverse Review Panels are Essential

Had Zomato's creative review included Dalit voices or caste studies experts, this oversight might have been caught before launch. Diverse perspectives in the creative process are not optional—they're essential for brand safety.
