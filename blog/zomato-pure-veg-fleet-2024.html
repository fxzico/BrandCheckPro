Case Study: Zomato Pure Veg Fleet Controversy (2024)

88%
Backlash Threat Score

In March 2024, food delivery aggregator Zomato announced the launch of a "Pure Veg Fleet" tailored specifically for users with strict vegetarian preferences. In addition to sourcing exclusively from vegetarian restaurants, the fleet’s delivery riders were slated to wear bright green uniforms instead of Zomato’s signature red. This visual segregation ignited an immediate national outcry regarding caste-based exclusion and delivery partner safety.

“Physical and visual segregation of gig workers along dietary preferences maps directly onto historical caste-based purity prejudices. Physical uniform changes represent a severe operational tripwire.”

1. Purity Politics and Partner Safety Concerns

While the initial design intended to satisfy customer demands regarding strict food hygiene and vegetarian preferences, the visual division of delivery agents triggered massive pushback. Critics argued that the green uniforms normalized discriminatory prejudices, reinforcing notions of "purity and pollution" closely tied to India's caste system.

Furthermore, gig worker unions and social commentators pointed out a severe threat to delivery partner safety. Visible markers indicating whether a driver carried non-vegetarian food could lead to landlords or housing associations blocking entry to "non-pure" red-uniformed riders, or exposing them to harassment and physical danger in highly conservative neighborhoods. This fell under **ASCI Chapter II (Decency & Discrimination)**.

2. Real-Time Policy Rollback

Within 11 hours of the launch announcement, Zomato CEO Deepinder Goyal posted a series of updates, rolling back the uniform requirement. While the digital "Pure Veg Mode" on the app remained, the company decided that all delivery partners would wear the uniform red color.

Goyal acknowledged the unintended consequences, noting that segregating uniforms could have stigmatized riders. The corporate takeaway is that product features cannot be designed in a socio-cultural vacuum; operational modifications must consider physical partner security and broader sociological impacts.
